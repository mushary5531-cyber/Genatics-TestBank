**Q1.** A patient is suspected to have sickle cell anemia. What is the
best cytomolecular technique to directly detect the HBB gene mutation?

A) Spectrophotometry (hemoglobin electrophoresis)
B) DNA sequencing (Sanger or NGS)
C) Complete blood count (CBC)
D) Western blot

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** To directly detect the specific HBB point mutation causing sickle cell anemia (c.20A>T, p.Glu7Val) at the molecular level, DNA sequencing (Sanger sequencing or targeted NGS) is the gold standard cytomolecular technique. It directly identifies the nucleotide change. Hemoglobin electrophoresis (spectrophotometry) detects the abnormal HbS protein indirectly. CBC shows anemia and sickle cells but is not a molecular technique. RFLP (restriction fragment length polymorphism with Mst II enzyme) is also used classically.

**Q2.** Which chromosomal testing method is most appropriate for
detecting a specific genetic disorder caused by a mutation on chromosome 4p?

A) Sanger sequencing
B) FISH (fluorescence in situ hybridization)
C) Conventional karyotyping
D) Whole genome sequencing

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** FISH uses fluorescently labeled DNA probes that hybridize to a specific chromosomal locus (such as chromosome 4p for Wolf-Hirschhorn syndrome). It can detect deletions, duplications, and translocations at specific known loci that may be too small to see on conventional karyotype. Sanger sequencing detects point mutations (not chromosomal deletions). Karyotyping detects large structural changes but misses microdeletions. WGS is comprehensive but FISH is the targeted, rapid choice for a known locus.

**Q3.** Which technique is most appropriate to detect a microdeletion at
chromosome 4p16.3 (Wolf-Hirschhorn syndrome)?

A) Conventional karyotyping
B) PCR alone
C) FISH
D) Southern blot

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Wolf-Hirschhorn syndrome is caused by a microdeletion at 4p16.3, typically too small (~1-2 Mb) to be detected by conventional karyotype. FISH using a specific probe for the WHSC1 region at 4p16.3 can detect this microdeletion. Chromosomal microarray (CMA) is now also commonly used. Conventional karyotype can only detect deletions >5-10 Mb. PCR amplifies sequences but cannot detect deletions without quantitative modification (like MLPA).

**Q4.** Which of the following mutation nomenclature represents a
frameshift mutation?

A) c.1258delC (p.Leu1258Phefs)
B) c.891+1G>A
C) c.742T>G
D) c.1003C>A

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Frameshift mutations result from insertions or deletions (indels) that shift the reading frame. c.1258delC = deletion of one cytosine at position 1258 → shifts the reading frame → p.Leu1258Phefs (frameshift starting at position 1258 with a new amino acid Phe, fs = frameshift). c.891+1G>A = splice site mutation (intronic). c.742T>G and c.1003C>A = missense mutations (single nucleotide substitutions that change one amino acid without shifting the frame).

**Q5.** The karyotype shown demonstrates an abnormality in the sex
chromosomes. What does this karyotype display?

A) Normal female (46,XX)
B) Turner syndrome with isochromosome (46,X,i(Xq))
C) Turner syndrome with ring chromosome
D) Turner syndrome with X deletion

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Turner syndrome (45,X) is the classic form, but ~80% of cases have a variant. An isochromosome of the long arm [i(Xq)] results in 46,X,i(Xq) — one normal X plus an isochromosome with two copies of the Xq (long) arm and deletion of the entire Xp (short) arm. This creates Turner syndrome phenotype (short stature, ovarian dysgenesis) because SHOX gene on Xp is deleted. Isochromosome Turner is the most common Turner variant on karyotype.

**Q6.** When identifying a gene mutation associated with hereditary
breast cancer risk, which genetic test would be most suitable?

A) FISH
B) Gene panel (NGS multi-gene panel)
C) Conventional karyotyping
D) Sanger sequencing of single gene

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Hereditary breast cancer can be caused by mutations in multiple genes (BRCA1, BRCA2, PALB2, CHEK2, ATM, CDH1, PTEN, STK11). A multi-gene NGS panel tests all relevant genes simultaneously from a single DNA sample, providing the most efficient and comprehensive diagnostic approach. FISH does not detect point mutations. Karyotype detects only large chromosomal abnormalities. Single-gene Sanger sequencing is appropriate when a specific known familial mutation is being tested in relatives.

**Q7.** Which of the following represents a pericentric inversion in a
female?

A) 46,XX,inv(6)(p15p18)
B) 46,XX,inv(6)(q15q18)
C) 46,XX,inv(6)(p15q18)
D) 46,XX,i(6)(p21p23)

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** A pericentric inversion involves a segment that INCLUDES the centromere — with breakpoints on both the short arm (p) AND the long arm (q). Notation: inv(chromosome)(p-breakpointq-breakpoint). 46,XX,inv(6)(p15q18) = female, chromosome 6, inversion with breakpoints at p15 (short arm) and q18 (long arm) — this CROSSES the centromere → pericentric. Option A: both breakpoints on p → paracentric. Option B: both on q → paracentric. Option D: i(6) = isochromosome, not inversion.

**Q8.** Which karyotype notation correctly represents a deletion of the
short arm of chromosome 10 in a male?

A) 46,XX,del(10)(p11.2p23)
B) 46,XY,del(10)(p11.2p23)
C) 45,XY,del(10)(q11.2q23)
D) 46,XY,del(10)(p11q23)

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Correct notation breakdown: 46 = total chromosome number; XY = male sex chromosomes; del(10) = deletion of chromosome 10; (p11.2p23) = breakpoints on the short arm (p) from band 11.2 to band 23, specifying the deleted segment. Option A: XX = female (wrong sex). Option C: 45 = wrong total (should be 46 for a simple deletion); q = long arm (not short arm). Option D: del(10)(p11q23) spans both arms → this would be an unbalanced rearrangement involving centromere.

**Q9.** PCR (polymerase chain reaction) amplifies DNA using which enzyme?

A) Taq DNA polymerase (thermostable)
B) Restriction endonuclease
C) Plasmid DNA polymerase
D) Phage lambda polymerase

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** PCR uses a thermostable DNA polymerase — most commonly Taq polymerase (from Thermus aquaticus). The reaction cycles through: denaturation (94°C) → annealing of primers (50-65°C) → extension by DNA polymerase (72°C). The thermostable enzyme is essential because it survives the high-temperature denaturation step without being destroyed. Restriction enzymes cut DNA at specific sequences — they do not amplify DNA. Plasmids are vectors, not enzymes.

**Q10.** Detection of proteins by Western blot relies primarily on:

A) Protein-antibody specificity alone
B) Labeling the antibody with a detectable dye/enzyme
C) Protein denaturation by SDS alone
D) Labeling the protein with dye before electrophoresis

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Western blot (immunoblot) procedure: (1) Proteins separated by SDS-PAGE (size-based, using protein denaturation), (2) transferred to nitrocellulose/PVDF membrane, (3) blocked, (4) incubated with primary antibody (specific for target protein), (5) secondary antibody (labeled with enzyme like HRP or fluorescent dye) binds the primary antibody, (6) detection reagent reveals the signal. The detection relies on the LABELED ANTIBODY — specifically the secondary antibody conjugated to an enzyme/dye. Both antibody specificity AND labeling are required, but the visualization step uses the labeled antibody.
