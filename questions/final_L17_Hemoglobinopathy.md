**Q1.** An adult hemoglobin molecule consists of:

A) 4 heme groups with 2 alpha chains and 2 beta chains
B) 4 heme groups with 2 alpha chains and 1 gamma chain
C) 3 heme groups with 2 alpha chains and 2 beta chains
D) 3 heme groups with 2 alpha chains and 1 gamma chain

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Adult hemoglobin A (HbA) consists of 4 globin chains: 2 alpha (α) chains + 2 beta (β) chains, each carrying one heme group (iron-containing porphyrin ring). Total: 4 heme groups + 4 globin chains. Fetal hemoglobin (HbF) = α2γ2. HbA2 (minor adult Hb) = α2δ2. Each heme group binds one oxygen molecule, so HbA can carry 4 O₂ molecules.

**Q2.** Which condition will occur if there is a loss (deletion) of all 4
alpha-globin genes?

A) Cooley's anemia (beta-thalassemia major)
B) Delta-beta thalassemia
C) HbH disease (3-gene deletion)
D) Hydrops fetalis (Hb Bart's disease)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Alpha-thalassemia severity correlates with the number of alpha-globin gene deletions: 1 gene deletion = silent carrier; 2 genes = alpha-thal trait; 3 genes = HbH disease (HbH = β4 tetramers); 4 genes = Hydrops fetalis / Hb Bart's disease (γ4 tetramers). HbH disease causes moderate hemolytic anemia. Hydrops fetalis (all 4 alpha genes deleted) causes severe fetal anemia, hydrops, and is usually fatal in utero or shortly after birth.

**Q3.** A molecular analysis shows a mutation of the triplet codon for
glutamic acid in the beta-globin gene. Which of the following hemoglobin
variants will be produced?

A) HbS (sickle cell hemoglobin)
B) HbC
C) HbF (fetal hemoglobin)
D) HbA2

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** HbS (sickle cell hemoglobin) is caused by a single missense mutation in the HBB gene: codon 6 changes from GAG (glutamic acid, Glu) to GTG (valine, Val). This is the classic glutamic acid codon mutation. The resulting βS chain forms HbS (α2βS2), which polymerizes under deoxygenation causing sickling. HbC results from Glu→Lys at the same codon 6. Note: some batches of this question in past exams listed the answer as HbA2 — however, the correct pharmacogenetic answer based on the slides is HbS.

**Q4.** A newborn has Hb Bart's detected on neonatal hemoglobin
screening. What is the composition of Hb Bart's?

A) γ4 (four gamma chains)
B) α2β2 (normal adult hemoglobin)
C) α2γ2 (fetal hemoglobin)
D) α2δ2 (HbA2)

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Hb Bart's (Hemoglobin Bart's) = γ4 — four gamma globin chains forming tetramers. It occurs when all 4 alpha-globin genes are deleted (Hb Bart's hydrops fetalis). Without alpha chains, excess gamma chains form unstable γ4 tetramers. Hb Bart's has very high oxygen affinity and cannot release oxygen to fetal tissues, causing severe fetal hypoxia, hydrops fetalis, and stillbirth.

**Q5.** Which of the following is a qualitative (structural) beta-globin
disorder?

A) Cooley's anemia (beta-thalassemia major)
B) Sickle cell anemia
C) Bart's hydrops fetalis
D) HbH disease

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Hemoglobin disorders are classified as: Quantitative (thalassemias) — reduced or absent production of a structurally normal globin chain (e.g., beta-thalassemia major = Cooley's anemia, HbH, Bart's hydrops). Qualitative (structural hemoglobinopathies) — structurally abnormal hemoglobin due to amino acid substitution (e.g., sickle cell anemia = HbS Glu→Val, HbC, HbE). Sickle cell anemia is the prototype qualitative beta-globin disorder.

**Q6.** A 2-year-old child presents with characteristic facial features
(prominent forehead, maxillary prominence), severe pallor, and has
required repeated blood transfusions since infancy. What is the
definitive cure for this condition?

A) Chelating agent therapy only
B) Bone marrow (hematopoietic stem cell) transplant
C) Hydroxyurea
D) Constant regular transfusions

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The description is beta-thalassemia major (Cooley's anemia): characterized by severe microcytic hemolytic anemia, transfusion dependence from infancy, and typical facial features from bone marrow expansion. The only definitive cure is allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT/bone marrow transplant), which replaces the defective hematopoietic stem cells. Chelation therapy manages iron overload from transfusions but is not curative. Hydroxyurea is used in sickle cell disease to induce HbF. Gene therapy (Zynteglo) is an emerging curative option.

**Q7.** One parent is affected with sickle cell disease (HbSS) and the
other parent has sickle cell trait (HbAS). What is the recurrence risk
for each pregnancy?

A) 50% chance of an affected child (HbSS)
B) 25% chance of an unaffected child (HbAA)
C) No counseling needed as risk is minimal
D) 75% chance of an affected child

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Cross: HbSS (affected) × HbAS (carrier/trait): All gametes from SS parent carry S. AS parent gives A or S (50% each). Results: 50% HbAS (sickle cell trait — carrier, usually asymptomatic) + 50% HbSS (sickle cell disease — affected). Therefore, 50% risk of an affected child per pregnancy. This couple requires thorough genetic counseling regarding recurrence risk and prenatal diagnosis options.
