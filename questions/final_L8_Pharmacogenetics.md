**Q1.** What is the underlying cause of dark urine, jaundice and low red
blood cell count and hemoglobin concentration after intake of
primaquine in a malaria patient?

A) Dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase deficiency
B) Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency
C) Cytochrome P450 deficiency
D) Thiopurine methyltransferase deficiency

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** G6PD deficiency is an X-linked recessive condition where the G6PD enzyme is deficient, impairing the hexose monophosphate shunt and reducing NADPH/glutathione in red blood cells. Oxidative drugs such as primaquine cause oxidative stress, leading to hemolysis. Classic presentation: hemolytic anemia with dark urine (hemoglobinuria) and jaundice after exposure to oxidizing triggers (primaquine, dapsone, fava beans, infections).

**Q2.** Using genetic or genomic information to select the most appropriate
choice of pharmacological therapy at the correct dosage for every person
is called:

A) Pharmacogenetics
B) Pharmacogenomics
C) Personalized medicine
D) Genetic variation

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Personalized medicine (also called precision medicine) uses genetic/genomic information to tailor drug choice and dosage to each individual patient. Pharmacogenetics refers specifically to single-gene effects on drug response. Pharmacogenomics is the broader study of how the entire genome influences drug response. Personalized medicine applies that knowledge clinically.

**Q3.** What mutation causes malignant hyperthermia?

A) Mutation in RYR1
B) Mutation in pCHE
C) Mutation in TPMT
D) Mutation in DPYD

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Malignant hyperthermia (MH) is a pharmacogenetic condition triggered by volatile anesthetics (e.g., halothane) and succinylcholine. It is caused by mutations in the RYR1 gene encoding the ryanodine receptor type 1 in skeletal muscle sarcoplasmic reticulum, leading to uncontrolled calcium release, severe hyperthermia, muscle rigidity, and rhabdomyolysis. MH follows autosomal dominant inheritance. Treatment: immediate dantrolene.

**Q4.** A patient experienced prolonged apnea after being given
succinylcholine during surgery. What enzyme do they have a deficiency in?

A) Pseudocholinesterase (butyrylcholinesterase)
B) Dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase
C) Thiopurine methyltransferase
D) G6PD

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Pseudocholinesterase (also called butyrylcholinesterase, encoded by BCHE) normally rapidly hydrolyzes succinylcholine. Individuals with BCHE variants have reduced enzyme activity, causing prolonged neuromuscular blockade and apnea after succinylcholine administration. This is inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern. Management: mechanical ventilation until drug is cleared.

**Q5.** Fast acetylators of isoniazid (used in tuberculosis treatment) usually suffer from:

A) High failure of isoniazid in treating tuberculosis
B) Drug-induced systemic lupus erythematosus
C) Increased risk of bladder cancer
D) Peripheral neuropathy

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** N-acetyltransferase 2 (NAT2) acetylates isoniazid. Fast acetylators rapidly inactivate isoniazid → lower plasma drug levels → subtherapeutic concentrations → increased risk of treatment failure for tuberculosis. Slow acetylators maintain higher drug levels and are at increased risk of drug-induced lupus erythematosus (DILE) and peripheral neuropathy from isoniazid accumulation.

**Q6.** The mutated gene responsible for neonatal diabetes is:

A) KCNJ11
B) TPMT
C) CFTR
D) HBB

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Permanent neonatal diabetes mellitus (PNDM) is most commonly caused by activating (gain-of-function) mutations in KCNJ11, which encodes the Kir6.2 subunit of the ATP-sensitive potassium (K-ATP) channel in pancreatic beta cells. Mutant channels remain open (fail to close in response to ATP from glucose metabolism), preventing insulin secretion. Importantly, PNDM due to KCNJ11 mutations can often be treated with oral sulfonylureas rather than insulin.

**Q7.** A male patient with tuberculosis is receiving isoniazid and develops
a severe adverse effect. What should be done?

A) Increase the dose of isoniazid
B) Continue treatment without stopping
C) Change the drug to an alternative
D) Add another antibiotic to the regimen

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** If a patient develops a severe adverse effect to isoniazid (such as hepatotoxicity, severe peripheral neuropathy, or drug-induced lupus in slow acetylators), the appropriate action is to discontinue isoniazid and switch to an alternative anti-tuberculosis regimen. Continuing or increasing the dose would worsen the adverse effect. Severe hepatotoxicity from isoniazid requires drug discontinuation.

**Q8.** The NAT2 (N-acetyltransferase 2) gene is related to the metabolism
of which drug?

A) Succinylcholine
B) G6PD-sensitive drugs
C) Isoniazid
D) Thiopurines

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** NAT2 (N-acetyltransferase 2) is the enzyme responsible for acetylation of isoniazid. NAT2 polymorphisms determine "fast" vs "slow" acetylator phenotype: Slow acetylators → higher isoniazid plasma levels → increased risk of peripheral neuropathy and drug-induced lupus erythematosus. Fast acetylators → lower drug levels → potential treatment failure for TB. BCHE metabolizes succinylcholine. TPMT metabolizes thiopurines. G6PD is unrelated to acetylation.

**Q9.** A patient receiving 5-fluorouracil (5-FU) chemotherapy develops
severe myelosuppression and mucositis after the first dose. Which enzyme
deficiency is most likely responsible for this severe toxicity?

A) CYP2D6
B) UGT1A1
C) DPYD (dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase)
D) G6PD

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** 5-FU is catabolized primarily by DPYD (dihydropyrimidine dehydrogenase). DPYD deficiency → reduced 5-FU catabolism → toxic drug accumulation → severe, potentially fatal toxicity (myelosuppression, severe mucositis, neurotoxicity). ~3–5% of the population has partial DPYD deficiency. DPYD genotyping before 5-FU therapy is now recommended in European guidelines. CYP2D6 is involved in codeine/tamoxifen metabolism. UGT1A1 affects irinotecan toxicity. G6PD deficiency causes oxidant-induced hemolysis.

**Q10.** A patient develops hemolytic anemia after eating fava beans
(favism). Which gene is responsible for this condition?

A) G6PD (glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase)
B) HBB (beta-globin)
C) CYP2D6
D) DPYD

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Favism is caused by G6PD (glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase) deficiency — the most common enzymopathy worldwide (X-linked recessive). G6PD is essential for protecting red blood cells from oxidative stress via the pentose phosphate pathway (NADPH production). Oxidant triggers (fava beans, primaquine, dapsone, sulfonamides, infections) → hemolytic anemia in G6PD-deficient individuals. HBB mutations cause hemoglobinopathies (sickle cell, thalassemia). CYP2D6/DPYD are drug-metabolizing enzymes.

**Q11.** Favism (hemolytic anemia triggered by eating fava beans) is
primarily caused by which genetic change?

A) G6PD gene mutation leading to enzyme deficiency
B) HBB gene mutation causing sickling under oxidative stress
C) TPMT deficiency causing thiopurine toxicity
D) NAT2 slow acetylation phenotype

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Favism results from G6PD gene mutation causing enzyme deficiency (X-linked recessive). G6PD deficiency impairs NADPH production → red blood cells cannot neutralize oxidative stress from fava beans (which contain vicine and convicine, potent oxidants) → hemolytic anemia. G6PD deficiency is most prevalent in malaria-endemic regions (confers some resistance to P. falciparum). It is the most common genetic cause of hemolytic anemia triggered by food or drugs.

**Q12.** A patient on warfarin anticoagulation develops excessive bleeding
despite standard dosing. Pharmacogenomic testing is most likely to reveal
a mutation in which gene?

A) CYP2D6
B) VKORC1 alone
C) CYP2C9
D) NAT2

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Warfarin is metabolized primarily by CYP2C9 (S-warfarin — the more potent enantiomer). CYP2C9 poor metabolizer variants (*2, *3) → reduced warfarin catabolism → drug accumulation → excessive anticoagulation and bleeding risk. VKORC1 variants affect warfarin target sensitivity and are also clinically relevant for dosing. Both CYP2C9 and VKORC1 genotyping are used in pharmacogenomics-guided warfarin dosing algorithms. CYP2D6 affects codeine/tamoxifen. NAT2 affects isoniazid.

**Q13.** Which of the following patients should NOT receive sulfonamide
antibiotics due to risk of acute hemolytic anemia?

A) Patient with CYP2D6 poor metabolizer genotype
B) Patient with G6PD deficiency
C) Patient with NAT2 slow acetylator status
D) Patient with sickle cell trait (HbAS)

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Sulfonamides are oxidant drugs that precipitate acute hemolytic anemia in G6PD-deficient patients. G6PD deficiency impairs NADPH production → insufficient glutathione reduction → failure to neutralize oxidative stress from sulfonamides → hemolysis. Sulfonamides are CONTRAINDICATED in G6PD deficiency. Other contraindicated oxidant agents include: primaquine, dapsone, nitrofurantoin, rasburicase. CYP2D6, NAT2, and sickle cell trait do not affect sulfonamide safety.
