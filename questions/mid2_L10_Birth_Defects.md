**Q1.** A newborn is diagnosed with an isolated congenital heart defect. Which
type of birth defect does this represent?

A) Dysplasia
B) Disruption
C) Malformation
D) Deformation

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** A malformation is a primary structural defect arising from an intrinsic abnormality in a developmental process (e.g., congenital heart defects, cleft lip/palate, neural tube defects). It is primary and intrinsic.

**Q2.** Which of the following birth defects is considered a disruption?

A) Congenital heart defect
B) Neural tube defect
C) Cleft lip and palate
D) Amniotic band syndrome

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** A disruption is a secondary and extrinsic birth defect caused by an external destructive force that breaks down or interferes with an otherwise normal developmental process. Amniotic bands can wrap around limbs and cause disruption. It is NOT genetic.

**Q3.** Certain malformations tend to occur together more frequently than
expected by chance. Which of the following best represents this concept?

A) Down syndrome
B) VACTERL association
C) Ectodermal dysplasia
D) Cleft lip and palate

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** An association is a non-random occurrence of multiple anomalies that are not a known syndrome or sequence. VACTERL is an example (Vertebral, Anal, Cardiac, Tracheo-Esophageal, Renal, Limb anomalies).

**Q4.** A newborn presents with vertebral anomalies, anal atresia,
tracheoesophageal fistula, and renal abnormalities. What is the most likely
diagnosis?

A) Potter sequence
B) Van der Woude syndrome
C) VATER association
D) Ectodermal dysplasia

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** VATER/VACTERL association includes: Vertebral anomalies, Anal atresia, Cardiac defects, Tracheo-Esophageal fistula, Renal anomalies, Limb defects. It has no known single genetic cause and normal WES/microarray.

**Q5.** A male newborn with vertebral anomalies, anal atresia, tracheoesophageal
fistula, and radial ray defects has normal WES and microarray results. What is
the most likely diagnosis?

A) Potter sequence
B) VATER association
C) Van der Woude syndrome
D) Ectodermal dysplasia

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** VATER association is non-random co-occurrence of structural anomalies with no identifiable genetic cause (normal chromosomes and microarray). The presence of radial ray defects is characteristic. This is an association, not a syndrome.

**Q6.** Elevated amniotic alpha fetoprotein (AFP) has been detected prenatally
in a patient. To reduce the risk of developing the consequence shown in the
photo, what could be given?

A) Vitamin B12
B) Folic acid
C) IgA
D) Immunosuppressant

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Elevated AFP indicates an open neural tube defect (NTD) such as anencephaly or spina bifida. Periconceptional folic acid supplementation significantly reduces the risk of NTDs. The image shows a meningomyelocele (spina bifida), the most common open NTD.

**Q7.** Which of the following terms describes the abnormal mechanical force
which distorts an otherwise normal structure?

A) Malformation
B) Disruption
C) Deformation
D) Dysplasia

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Deformation is caused by abnormal mechanical forces (extrinsic) that distort an otherwise normally formed structure. Examples include clubfoot (talipes equinovarus) from uterine constraint, and positional plagiocephaly. It differs from disruption in that the structure was initially normal.

**Q8.** What is the best description of disruption?

A) Primary and extrinsic
B) Secondary and extrinsic
C) Primary and intrinsic
D) Secondary and intrinsic

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Disruption is secondary (the original structure was forming normally) and extrinsic (caused by an outside destructive factor such as amniotic bands, infection, or ischemia). Malformation, by contrast, is primary and intrinsic.

**Q9.** What is correct regarding neural tube defects?

A) Anencephaly results from a defect in the lower end of the neural tube
B) The risk can be reduced by periconceptional folic acid supplementation
C) The recurrence risk from an affected parent is 10%
D) Spina bifida results from failure of cranial neural tube closure

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Periconceptional folic acid supplementation (before and during early pregnancy) reduces NTD risk by 50-70%. Anencephaly results from failure of CRANIAL (anterior) neural tube closure. Spina bifida results from failure of CAUDAL (posterior) closure. Recurrence risk is approximately 2-3%, not 10%.

**Q10.** Which of the following is caused by abnormal organization of tissue?

A) Ectodermal dysplasia
B) Spina bifida
C) Cleft palate
D) Talipes

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Dysplasia refers to abnormal organization or function of cells within a specific tissue type. Ectodermal dysplasia results from abnormal development of ectodermal tissue (affecting hair, skin, teeth, nails, sweat glands). Most dysplasias are due to single gene defects.

**Q11.** A family came with their 7-year-old girl and 5-year-old boy, complaining
of chronic pulmonary disease, failure to thrive with malnutrition, and chronic
diarrhea due to pancreatic insufficiency. What other clinical feature might
be seen?

A) Male infertility due to testicular atrophy
B) Male infertility due to congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens
C) Female infertility due to ovarian failure
D) Infertility due to primary ciliary dyskinesia

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The clinical picture describes cystic fibrosis (CF) — autosomal recessive CFTR mutation. Males with CF frequently have congenital bilateral absence of the vas deferens (CBAVD), causing obstructive azoospermia and infertility. Testicular function is normal. Females with CF have reduced fertility due to thick cervical mucus, but not ovarian failure.

**Q12.** Which medication, if given in the first trimester of pregnancy, helps
reduce the risk of neural tube defects?

A) Streptomycin
B) Folic acid
C) Isoniazid
D) Valproic acid

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Folic acid (vitamin B9) given periconceptionally and in the first trimester prevents neural tube defects (NTDs) by supporting proper neural tube closure. Streptomycin is ototoxic (teratogen). Isoniazid and valproic acid are potentially harmful teratogens; valproic acid is actually associated with INCREASED risk of NTDs.

**Q13.** The image shows a newborn with a protrusion of abdominal contents through the umbilicus, covered by a thin membrane. What type of birth defect does this represent and what is the correct term for this finding?

A) Disruption — gastroschisis
B) Malformation — omphalocele
C) Deformation — umbilical hernia
D) Dysplasia — abdominal wall dystrophy

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Omphalocele is a malformation (primary, intrinsic defect) where abdominal organs herniate through the umbilical ring and are covered by a peritoneal membrane. It differs from gastroschisis (a disruption) where organs herniate through a defect lateral to the umbilicus with NO covering membrane. Omphalocele is associated with chromosomal abnormalities (trisomy 13, 18) and BWS syndrome.

**Q14.** An adult male presents with the physical findings shown in the image (gynecomastia, sparse body hair, and hypogonadism). These features developed as a result of a congenital chromosomal abnormality. What is the most likely underlying karyotype?

A) 46,XY (Normal male)
B) 45,X (Turner syndrome)
C) 47,XXY (Klinefelter syndrome)
D) 47,XYY (Jacob syndrome)

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The image shows an adult male with gynecomastia and sparse body/facial hair — classic features of Klinefelter syndrome (47,XXY). Although it is a congenital chromosomal condition (present from birth), many clinical manifestations only become apparent during or after puberty. Features include: tall stature, small firm testes, azoospermia, infertility, gynecomastia, female-distribution pubic hair, and learning difficulties. It is the most common sex chromosome aneuploidy in males.

**Q15.** A newborn presents with macroglossia (large tongue), macrosomia (large birth weight), and abdominal wall defect. The infant also develops severe hypoglycemia in the first hours of life. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A) Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
B) Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome
C) VACTERL association
D) Turner syndrome

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The image shows a newborn with Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome (BWS): an overgrowth syndrome characterized by macroglossia, macrosomia, omphalocele/abdominal wall defects, and neonatal hypoglycemia. BWS is caused by dysregulation of imprinted genes at chromosome 11p15.5 (IGF2/H19 region). Risk of embryonal tumors (Wilms tumor, hepatoblastoma) is increased. Most cases are sporadic; ~15% are familial.
