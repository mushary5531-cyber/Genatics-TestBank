**Q1.** An image shows an infant with characteristic facial features
including upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthal folds, flat nasal
bridge, and single palmar crease. What chromosomal abnormality does
this infant have?

A) Trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome)
B) Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome)
C) Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome)
D) Monosomy X (Turner syndrome)

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The described features — upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthal folds, flat nasal bridge, single palmar (Simian) crease, hypotonia, and brachycephaly — are classic features of Down syndrome (Trisomy 21). Down syndrome is the most common chromosomal aneuploidy, caused by nondisjunction (95%), Robertsonian translocation (4%), or mosaicism (1%). It is the most common genetic cause of intellectual disability.

**Q2.** An 8-year-old boy presents with intellectual disability, a long
face, broad forehead, large ears, and a prominent mandible. His
maternal uncle is similarly affected. What is the most likely indication
confirming this condition?

A) Karyotype showing 47,XXY
B) G6PD enzyme deficiency
C) Abnormal increase in CGG trinucleotide repeats in the FMR1 gene
D) Elevated plasma phenylalanine

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The description is classic Fragile X syndrome — the most common inherited cause of intellectual disability. Features: large ears, prominent jaw, long face, macro-orchidism (post-pubertal), intellectual disability, and behavioral issues (autism spectrum). The pattern of inheritance is X-linked (maternal uncle affected). Fragile X is caused by expansion of CGG trinucleotide repeats (>200 repeats = full mutation) in the 5' UTR of the FMR1 gene on Xq27.3, leading to gene silencing via methylation.

**Q3.** A child is suspected to have Prader-Willi syndrome. Which
genetic testing method is required to confirm the diagnosis?

A) Conventional chromosomal karyotype
B) Methylation analysis (methylation-specific PCR or methylation array)
C) Whole exome sequencing
D) FISH for chromosome 15q11-q13

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Prader-Willi syndrome (PWS) and Angelman syndrome (AS) both involve chromosome 15q11-q13 but result from loss of different parental imprints. PWS = loss of PATERNAL 15q11-q13 expression (deletion, maternal UPD15, or imprinting center defect). AS = loss of MATERNAL 15q11-q13 expression. Methylation analysis detects the imprinting status of the region and confirms PWS/AS regardless of the underlying mechanism (deletion, UPD, or imprinting defect). Karyotype and FISH may miss UPD and imprinting defects.

**Q4.** A 6-year-old boy presents with hypogonadism, obesity, and
almond-shaped eyes. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A) Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
B) Fragile X syndrome
C) Angelman syndrome
D) Prader-Willi syndrome

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Prader-Willi syndrome (PWS) classic features: neonatal hypotonia and feeding difficulties, followed by hyperphagia and obesity in childhood, hypogonadism (small genitalia, undescended testes), almond-shaped eyes, short stature, intellectual disability, and behavioral problems (skin-picking, tantrums). PWS is caused by loss of paternal 15q11-q13 expression (deletion, maternal UPD15, or imprinting defect). Confirmed by methylation analysis. Angelman syndrome = loss of maternal 15q11-q13 (happy demeanor, seizures, absent speech).

**Q5.** A patient has aortic aneurysm and arachnodactyly (spider fingers).
Which syndrome is most likely?

A) Down syndrome
B) Marfan syndrome
C) Prader-Willi syndrome
D) Turner syndrome

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Marfan syndrome (FBN1 mutation, autosomal dominant) classically presents with: aortic root dilation/aneurysm (life-threatening complication — rupture/dissection), ectopia lentis (upward lens dislocation), arachnodactyly, tall stature with long limbs (dolichostenomelia), pectus excavatum/carinatum, and scoliosis. Aortic root dilation is the most serious manifestation. Regular echocardiographic surveillance is essential. Beta-blockers reduce progression of aortic dilation.

**Q6.** A patient has duodenal atresia and a wide gap between the 1st
and 2nd toes (sandal gap). Which syndrome is most likely?

A) Marfan syndrome
B) Prader-Willi syndrome
C) Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
D) Neurofibromatosis type 1

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) features include: sandal gap (wide space between 1st and 2nd toes), single palmar crease, Brushfield spots, upslanting palpebral fissures, epicanthal folds, brachycephaly, and hypotonia. Duodenal atresia ("double bubble" sign on X-ray) is a well-known association with Down syndrome (30% of duodenal atresia cases have Trisomy 21). Hirschsprung disease and Alzheimer's disease (early onset) are also associated.

**Q7.** A 16-year-old female presents with short stature and primary
amenorrhea. Chromosomal analysis shows only 45 chromosomes. Which
karyotype is most consistent?

A) 45,XX,t(14;21)
B) 45,XX,rob(21;22)
C) 46,X,isoXq
D) 45,X (monosomy X)

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Wait - the answer from the exam key is C: 46,X,isoXq. The question states 45 chromosomes but the listed answer C shows 46. This is a variant of Turner syndrome: 46,X,i(Xq) has 46 chromosomes total (1 normal X + 1 isochromosome Xq), giving Turner phenotype due to Xp deletion. Short stature (SHOX gene on Xp is deleted) and primary amenorrhea (streak gonads) are typical Turner features. NOTE: The answer key from the exam states C (46,X,isoXq). Options A and B describe Robertsonian translocations (associated with trisomy 21/13 risk, not Turner).

**Q8.** A boy 2 months old has dysmorphic features, severe hypotonia,
liver and renal cysts. What is the most appropriate investigation?

A) DNA analysis of SMN1 gene
B) DNA analysis of DMD (dystrophin) gene
C) Chromosomal studies (karyotype)
D) Evaluation of serum very long chain fatty acids (VLCFA)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** The combination of severe neonatal hypotonia + dysmorphic features + hepatomegaly + renal cysts is classic for Zellweger syndrome (peroxisomal biogenesis disorder). Zellweger is diagnosed by markedly elevated plasma very long chain fatty acids (VLCFA) — peroxisomes normally degrade VLCFA by beta-oxidation. SMN1 analysis is for SMA (floppy infant without organ abnormalities). Dystrophin analysis is for DMD (presents in early childhood, not neonates). Karyotype identifies chromosomal abnormalities (not Zellweger).

**Q9.** A girl 8 years old presents with short stature, webbed neck, and
increased carrying angle (cubitus valgus). Which investigation is most
appropriate?

A) DNA analysis of dystrophin gene
B) DNA analysis of CFTR gene
C) Chromosomal studies (karyotype)
D) DNA analysis of FGFR3 gene

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Short stature + webbed neck + cubitus valgus (increased carrying angle) = classic Turner syndrome (45,X or variant). Turner syndrome is a chromosomal abnormality → diagnosed by chromosomal analysis (karyotype). Dystrophin = DMD (muscle disease). CFTR = Cystic fibrosis (pulmonary/GI). FGFR3 = Achondroplasia (short-limbed dwarfism with rhizomelia). Turner features also include: low posterior hairline, shield chest, coarctation of the aorta, ovarian dysgenesis, and lymphedema.

**Q10.** A child with Down syndrome has a karyotype showing only
46 chromosomes (not 47). Chromosome analysis reveals a Robertsonian
translocation. The extra chromosome 21 material is most commonly
attached to which chromosome?

A) Chromosome 13
B) Chromosome 21
C) Chromosome 22
D) Chromosome 14

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Robertsonian translocation Down syndrome accounts for ~4% of Down syndrome cases. The extra chromosome 21 material is most commonly fused to chromosome 14 [rob(14;21)], resulting in a carrier karyotype of 45 chromosomes (45,XX/XY,rob(14;21)) but with phenotypically normal carriers. Offspring who receive both the translocation chromosome and a free chromosome 21 have 3 copies of chromosome 21 material with only 46 total chromosomes. Other Robertsonian partners include chromosomes 13, 15, 21, and 22, but chromosome 14 is the most common partner.

**Q11.** A parent is affected with neurofibromatosis type 1 (NF1), an
autosomal dominant condition. What is the probability that each of their
children will inherit the NF1 mutation?

A) 50% (1 in 2 chance per child)
B) 25% (1 in 4 chance)
C) 100% (all children will be affected)
D) 75% (3 in 4 chance)

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** NF1 (neurofibromatosis type 1, NF1 gene mutation) follows autosomal dominant inheritance. An affected parent (Aa) × unaffected parent (aa): 50% Aa (affected) + 50% aa (unaffected). Each child has a 50% independent probability of inheriting the NF1 mutation. Note: NF1 has high penetrance (~100%) but extremely variable expressivity (café-au-lait spots to severe complications). Also, ~50% of NF1 cases are de novo mutations with no family history — these carry the same 50% transmission risk to future children.

**Q12.** In a pedigree showing X-linked recessive inheritance, an
unaffected female (II-2) has an affected father and one affected son.
What is her genetic status?

A) Obligate carrier (XᴬXᵃ)
B) Definitely affected
C) Homozygous normal (XᴬXᴬ)
D) Genetic status cannot be determined

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** An unaffected female who has BOTH an affected father AND an affected son is an obligate carrier in X-linked recessive inheritance. From her affected father (XᵃY): she must have inherited his Xᵃ allele → she is XᴬXᵃ (carrier). She then passed the Xᵃ to her affected son (XᵃY). An obligate carrier is defined by pedigree logic — we can determine she MUST carry the allele regardless of molecular testing. Obligate carriers in X-linked recessive pedigrees: (1) daughters of affected males, (2) mothers of multiple affected sons, (3) females with affected fathers AND affected sons.

**Q13.** Paternal uniparental disomy of chromosome 15 (both chromosome
15 copies inherited from the father) leads to which condition?

A) Prader-Willi syndrome
B) Down syndrome
C) Angelman syndrome
D) Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Chromosome 15q11-q13 is subject to genomic imprinting. UBE3A (maternally expressed) is responsible for Angelman syndrome when maternal expression is lost. Paternal UPD15 = both chromosome 15s from father → no maternal chromosome 15 → no maternal UBE3A expression → Angelman syndrome (seizures, absent speech, happy puppet-like demeanor). Maternal UPD15 = both from mother → no paternal 15q11-q13 expression → Prader-Willi syndrome (hypotonia, hyperphagia, obesity). PWS and AS affect the SAME chromosomal region but differ in parent of origin.

**Q14.** A child with Down syndrome (Trisomy 21) presents with bilious
vomiting from birth. Abdominal X-ray shows a "double bubble" sign. Which
additional Down syndrome-associated anomaly is shown?

A) Hirschsprung disease
B) Duodenal atresia and atrial septal defect (ASD)
C) Esophageal atresia
D) Pyloric stenosis

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The "double bubble" sign on X-ray is pathognomonic of duodenal atresia (dilated stomach + proximal duodenum). ~30% of duodenal atresia cases are associated with Down syndrome (Trisomy 21). Down syndrome major structural anomalies include: congenital heart defects (ASD, VSD, AV canal/AVSD — most common cardiac defect), duodenal atresia, Hirschsprung disease, and imperforate anus. ASD (atrial septal defect) is a frequently associated cardiac defect in Down syndrome. This question asks about structural anomalies beyond the chromosomal abnormality.

**Q15.** Holoprosencephaly (failure of the forebrain to divide) is
associated with mutations in which gene?

A) NF1
B) FBN1
C) SHH (sonic hedgehog)
D) PAX6

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** SHH (Sonic Hedgehog) is a key developmental signaling molecule critical for midline forebrain development and separation. Loss-of-function mutations in SHH (or its pathway components) cause holoprosencephaly — failure of the embryonic forebrain (prosencephalon) to divide into two cerebral hemispheres. Clinical spectrum: alobar (most severe, single-lobed brain), semilobar, lobar. Associated features include midline facial defects (cyclopia, proboscis, hypotelorism, cleft lip/palate). Trisomy 13 is the most common chromosomal cause.

**Q16.** Decreased distance between the orbits (reduced interorbital
distance) is termed:

A) Hypotelorism
B) Hypertelorism
C) Telecanthus
D) Enophthalmos

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Hypotelorism = decreased interorbital distance (orbits closer together than normal). It is associated with midline developmental defects including holoprosencephaly (SHH mutations, Trisomy 13). Hypertelorism = INCREASED interorbital distance (orbits further apart) — seen in Noonan syndrome, Wolf-Hirschhorn syndrome. Telecanthus = increased distance between inner canthi without true hypertelorism. Enophthalmos = posterior displacement of the eyeball within the orbit.

**Q17.** In a pedigree, a condition is observed only in males, who
transmit it to ALL of their daughters but NONE of their sons. What is
the inheritance pattern?

A) Autosomal dominant
B) X-linked dominant
C) Autosomal recessive
D) X-linked recessive

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** X-linked dominant inheritance: The gene is on the X chromosome, and the mutant allele is dominant. Affected father (XᴬY): passes his X (with mutation) to all daughters → all daughters affected; passes Y to all sons → no sons affected. This "father-to-all-daughters, no sons" transmission is the hallmark of X-linked dominant inheritance from an affected father. Examples: Rett syndrome (MECP2), Incontinentia pigmenti (IKBKG), vitamin D-resistant rickets (PHEX).

**Q18.** A newborn presents with polydactyly (extra fingers/toes),
cleft lip/palate, and severe brain malformations. Which chromosomal
condition is most likely?

A) Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome)
B) Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome)
C) Trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome)
D) Monosomy X (Turner syndrome)

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome) classic features: polydactyly (postaxial), cleft lip AND palate, holoprosencephaly/midline brain defects, microphthalmia, cutis aplasia (scalp skin defects), and severe cardiac defects. It is the most severe autosomal trisomy compatible with live birth, with very poor prognosis (median survival <1 week). Trisomy 21 = Down syndrome (intellectual disability, characteristic facies — no polydactyly). Trisomy 18 = Edwards syndrome (overlapping fingers, rocker-bottom feet, omphalocele — no polydactyly).

**Q19.** A female presents with Turner syndrome phenotype (short stature,
primary amenorrhea). Karyotype analysis shows 46 chromosomes, one X, and
one ring chromosome. This ring X chromosome results in a variant of:

A) Klinefelter syndrome
B) Down syndrome
C) Turner syndrome
D) Fragile X syndrome

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Turner syndrome can result from various X-chromosome abnormalities beyond classic 45,X monosomy. A ring X chromosome [46,X,r(X)] forms when both ends of an X chromosome break and rejoin, deleting terminal sequences. If the ring lacks XIST (X-inactivation gene), the ring X cannot be inactivated → multiple genes expressed → severe phenotype. Ring X Turner phenotype: short stature, ovarian failure, and sometimes more severe features than classic 45,X. Classic Turner: 45,X (most common); variants: 46,X,i(Xq), 46,X,del(Xp), 46,X,r(X), and mosaics.

**Q20.** A 16-year-old tall male with long limbs, arm span greater than
height, and ectopia lentis (anterior lens dislocation) is referred for
genetic evaluation. Which molecular test is most diagnostic?

A) Chromosomal karyotype
B) Plasma amino acid analysis (homocysteine level)
C) Urine reducing substances
D) Echocardiography (functional)
E) DNA analysis of FBN1 gene (fibrillin-1)

**Answer:** E
**Explanation:** The presentation — tall stature, long limbs, arm span > height (dolichostenomelia), and lens dislocation — suggests a connective tissue disorder. FBN1 gene analysis (fibrillin-1, chromosome 15q21) is the molecular diagnostic test for Marfan syndrome. However, NOTE: anterior (forward) lens dislocation is more characteristic of homocystinuria (CBS deficiency), while Marfan syndrome classically shows SUPEROTEMPORAL (upward) dislocation. Given the Marfanoid habitus and the exam answer key identifying FBN1, Marfan syndrome is the primary diagnosis here.

**Q21.** A family pedigree shows a condition affecting only individuals
who inherit the trait through the MATERNAL line — affected males do NOT
transmit the condition to their children. What is the most likely
inheritance pattern?

A) Mitochondrial (maternal) inheritance
B) X-linked recessive
C) Autosomal dominant
D) Genomic imprinting

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Mitochondrial inheritance: Mitochondria are inherited ONLY from the mother (via the oocyte cytoplasm). All children of an affected MOTHER are at risk; no children of an affected FATHER are at risk (because sperm contribute no mitochondria to the embryo). This strict maternal-only transmission is the hallmark distinguishing mitochondrial inheritance from all other patterns. Examples: MELAS, MERRF, Leber hereditary optic neuropathy (LHON), Pearson syndrome. Note: mitochondrial disorders may show heteroplasmy (variable proportions of mutant mtDNA).

**Q22.** A child presents with low-set ears, micrognathia (small jaw),
a prenatal history of severe oligohydramnios, and bilateral talipes
equinovarus (clubfoot). The cause of the talipes is most likely:

A) A single-gene mutation affecting bone development
B) A chromosomal deletion causing limb malformation
C) Abnormal mechanical force causing deformation of normal feet
D) Teratogen exposure causing disruption of limb bud development

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Talipes equinovarus (clubfoot) occurring with severe oligohydramnios is a deformation (not malformation). Deformations result from abnormal mechanical forces on structurally normal tissue. Severe oligohydramnios (Potter sequence) → reduced amniotic fluid → uterine wall compresses fetus → abnormal positioning → talipes deformity (positional clubfoot), limb contractures, characteristic facial features. The underlying tissue is normal; the shape is distorted by physical compression. Malformations involve intrinsic developmental errors; disruptions involve destruction of previously normal tissue.

**Q23.** The karyotype of a male shows 47,XXY. What is the most likely
diagnosis?

A) Klinefelter syndrome
B) Turner syndrome
C) Down syndrome
D) XYY syndrome

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** 47,XXY = Klinefelter syndrome — the most common sex chromosome aneuploidy in males (1/500 males). Features: tall stature, small testes (hypogonadism), azoospermia/infertility, gynecomastia, reduced secondary sexual characteristics, mild intellectual impairment (some individuals). Usually not diagnosed until puberty or during infertility workup. Caused by nondisjunction during meiosis (maternal in 50%, paternal in 50%). Turner syndrome = 45,X (female). Down syndrome = 47,+21. XYY syndrome = 47,XYY.

**Q24.** Marfan syndrome is an example of which genetic phenomenon —
one gene causing multiple, apparently unrelated clinical features
(aortic aneurysm, ectopia lentis, tall stature, scoliosis)?

A) Pleiotropy
B) Variable expressivity
C) Incomplete penetrance
D) Genetic heterogeneity

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Pleiotropy is when a single gene mutation causes multiple, seemingly unrelated effects in different organ systems. Marfan syndrome (FBN1 mutation, fibrillin-1 protein) is the classic example: FBN1 is expressed throughout connective tissue → mutations affect multiple systems: cardiovascular (aortic aneurysm), ocular (ectopia lentis), skeletal (tall stature, arachnodactyly, scoliosis), and others. All diverse manifestations arise from a single genetic change. Variable expressivity = same gene, different severity. Genetic heterogeneity = same phenotype caused by mutations in different genes.

**Q25.** A consanguineous couple (first cousins) is concerned about
their risk of having a child with an autosomal recessive condition.
Their family has a history of cystic fibrosis (CF). What is the main
reason why consanguinity increases the risk of autosomal recessive
disease?

A) Consanguineous couples are more likely to carry the same autosomal recessive allele inherited from a common ancestor
B) Consanguinity always causes new de novo mutations
C) All first-cousin children are affected with recessive disease
D) Consanguinity increases dominant gene penetrance

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Consanguineous couples (related individuals) share portions of their genome inherited from a common ancestor. If a common ancestor was a carrier for an autosomal recessive condition (e.g., CFTR mutation for cystic fibrosis), multiple family members may have inherited the same mutant allele. When two carriers of the same allele reproduce, the risk of an affected child (homozygous) = 25%. The closer the relationship, the greater the probability of sharing identical-by-descent alleles. This is why consanguinity significantly increases the risk for autosomal recessive disorders.

**Q26.** Premarital screening programs include testing for which of the
following conditions? All EXCEPT:

A) Sickle cell disease
B) Beta-thalassemia
C) G6PD deficiency
D) Phenylketonuria (PKU)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Premarital genetic screening programs (common in Saudi Arabia and other Gulf countries) focus on conditions with high carrier frequency in the population where both partners being carriers creates significant reproductive risk. Standard premarital screening in Saudi Arabia includes: sickle cell disease (HbSS), beta-thalassemia, and other hemoglobinopathies. PKU (phenylketonuria) is NOT typically included in premarital screening programs — it is detected by newborn screening (Guthrie test). PKU is autosomal recessive but has different population prevalence and management pathway.
