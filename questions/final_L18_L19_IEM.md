**Q1.** Which of the following is considered a second-line (confirmatory)
test for inborn errors of metabolism?

A) Ammonia level
B) Lactic acid level
C) Blood gas
D) Urine organic acid analysis

**Answer:** D
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** IEM investigations are divided into: First-line (initial screening): ammonia, lactic acid, blood gas, glucose, CBC. Second-line (specific confirmatory): urine organic acids (identifies specific organic acidemias like methylmalonic acidemia, propionic acidemia), plasma amino acids (amino acidopathies), acylcarnitine profile (fatty acid oxidation defects), enzyme assays, and molecular testing. Urine organic acid analysis is a key second-line test that identifies the specific metabolic defect.

**Q2.** Which of the following conditions presents very similarly to
Marfan syndrome clinically?

A) Phenylketonuria
B) Hurler disease (MPS type I)
C) Homocystinuria
D) Tay-Sachs disease

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Homocystinuria (CBS deficiency — autosomal recessive) can closely mimic Marfan syndrome: tall stature, long limbs (dolichostenomelia), arachnodactyly, pectus deformity, scoliosis, and lens dislocation (ectopia lentis). Key differences: Marfan has UPWARD lens dislocation, homocystinuria has DOWNWARD dislocation; homocystinuria causes intellectual disability, thromboembolic events, and osteoporosis; Marfan does NOT. Homocystinuria is caused by CBS gene mutations.

**Q3.** Which of the following conditions is related to lysosomal storage
disorders?

A) Zellweger spectrum disorder
B) Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS)
C) Mitochondrial fatty acid oxidation defects
D) Peroxisomal biogenesis disorders

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Lysosomal storage disorders (LSDs) result from deficiencies of lysosomal enzymes leading to substrate accumulation within lysosomes. Mucopolysaccharidoses (MPS) — such as Hurler (MPS I), Hunter (MPS II), Morquio (MPS IV), and Maroteaux-Lamy (MPS VI) — are LSDs caused by deficiencies of enzymes that degrade glycosaminoglycans (mucopolysaccharides). Zellweger is a peroxisomal disorder (not lysosomal). Fatty acid oxidation disorders are mitochondrial.

**Q4.** A 6-year-old girl presents with intellectual disability, seizures,
decreased pigmentation of skin, hair, and iris, and a musty/mousy odor
of urine. Which amino acid would you expect to be elevated on
quantitative plasma amino acid profile?

A) Glycine
B) Homocysteine
C) Phenylalanine
D) Tyrosine

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** The classic presentation is Phenylketonuria (PKU) — autosomal recessive deficiency of phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH). Without PAH, phenylalanine (Phe) accumulates in blood and tissues → elevated plasma phenylalanine. Excess Phe is converted to phenylpyruvate/phenylacetate (musty/mousy odor). Phe competes with tyrosine for melanin synthesis → reduced skin/hair/iris pigmentation. Untreated PKU → severe intellectual disability, seizures, microcephaly.

**Q5.** A female patient has short stature and rhizomelic (proximal)
shortening of her limbs. Genetic testing shows a heterozygous FGFR3
mutation. If she marries a man with the same heterozygous mutation,
what percentage of their children will be unaffected (normal)?

A) 100%
B) 1-3%
C) 25%
D) 50%

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** The condition is achondroplasia (autosomal dominant FGFR3 mutation). Cross: Aa (affected) × Aa (affected): AA = 25% (homozygous achondroplasia — lethal in utero/early infancy), Aa = 50% (affected with achondroplasia), aa = 25% (unaffected/normal stature). Therefore, 25% of all pregnancies will result in an unaffected child. Note: among live-born children, the fraction of unaffected children is about 1/3 (since AA is typically lethal).

**Q6.** In ornithine transcarbamylase (OTC) deficiency, which
investigation finding is most characteristic?

A) Reduced orotic acid in urine
B) Elevated lactic acid
C) Hyperammonemia
D) Increased methionine and homocysteine

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** OTC deficiency is the most common urea cycle disorder (X-linked recessive). OTC catalyzes the conversion of ornithine + carbamoyl phosphate → citrulline. Deficiency → carbamoyl phosphate accumulates → diverted to pyrimidine synthesis → increased OROTIC ACID in urine (elevated, not reduced). Most characteristic finding: hyperammonemia (elevated NH3) due to failure of the urea cycle. Presentation: vomiting, lethargy, encephalopathy in neonates (severe form) or periodic episodes in mild form.

**Q7.** What is the best course of action for a 2-week-old neonate
diagnosed with galactosemia?

A) Lactose-free and galactose-free milk formula
B) Fructose-free milk
C) Liver transplant
D) Stem cell therapy

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 24
**Explanation:** Classic galactosemia (GALT deficiency — autosomal recessive) results in accumulation of galactose-1-phosphate. Treatment: immediate elimination of galactose and lactose from diet. Lactose (milk sugar) = glucose + galactose. Standard formula must be replaced with lactose-free AND galactose-free formula (soy-based or casein hydrolysate). Breastfeeding must stop. Early dietary treatment prevents liver damage, cataracts, and intellectual disability. Complications include E. coli sepsis in neonates.

**Q8.** A newborn presents with vomiting, lethargy, and elevated
phenylalanine levels. If the incidence of this condition is 1/10,000,
what is the carrier frequency?

A) 1:100
B) 1:25
C) 1:50
D) 1:10

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 24
**Explanation:** Using Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: disease frequency (q²) = 1/10,000. Therefore q = 1/100 (allele frequency of mutant allele). p ≈ 1 (since q is small). Carrier frequency = 2pq ≈ 2 × (1) × (1/100) = 2/100 = 1/50. The answer is 1:50. This principle applies to autosomal recessive conditions — carrier frequency is approximately 2√(disease incidence).

**Q9.** A neonate presents with vomiting, lethargy, and laboratory tests
reveal significantly increased plasma levels of valine, leucine, and
isoleucine. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A) Propionic acidemia
B) Maple syrup urine disease (MSUD)
C) Methylmalonic acidemia
D) Isovaleric acidemia

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 24
**Explanation:** Maple Syrup Urine Disease (MSUD) is caused by deficiency of branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase (BCKD). This enzyme catabolizes branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs): valine, leucine, and isoleucine. Deficiency → accumulation of BCAAs and their alpha-keto acids → sweet/maple syrup odor of urine → encephalopathy, seizures, cerebral edema. Treatment: BCAA-restricted diet, thiamine supplementation.

**Q10.** A 2-year-old child presents with intellectual disability and
hypopigmentation of hair. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A) Phenylketonuria (PKU)
B) Galactosemia
C) Homocystinuria
D) Tay-Sachs disease

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** PKU (phenylalanine hydroxylase deficiency) presents with: intellectual disability (untreated), fair skin and hair hypopigmentation (phenylalanine competes with tyrosine in melanin synthesis → reduced melanin → lighter pigmentation), musty urine odor, seizures, and eczema. Newborn screening (Guthrie test) detects elevated phenylalanine. Early dietary restriction of phenylalanine prevents neurological damage.

**Q11.** Which of the following is a characteristic clinical feature of
homocystinuria?

A) Sweet-smelling urine
B) Dark urine
C) Lens dislocation (ectopia lentis)
D) Short stature and skeletal dysplasia

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** Homocystinuria (CBS — cystathionine beta-synthase deficiency) features include: Marfanoid body habitus (tall, long limbs, arachnodactyly), ectopia lentis (lens dislocation — characteristically DOWNWARD, unlike Marfan's which is upward), intellectual disability, thromboembolic events (stroke, DVT), and osteoporosis. Sweet-smelling urine is characteristic of MSUD. Dark urine is seen in alkaptonuria.

**Q12.** What is the characteristic clinical feature of PKU on
physical examination?

A) Hair hypopigmentation
B) Sweet-smelling urine
C) Lens dislocation
D) Hepatomegaly

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** In PKU, excess phenylalanine competitively inhibits tyrosinase, reducing melanin synthesis → hypopigmentation of hair (blond/light hair even in dark-haired families), skin, and eyes. The urine has a musty/mousy odor (phenylacetate), NOT sweet. Sweet-smelling urine is MSUD. Lens dislocation is homocystinuria. Hepatomegaly is galactosemia.

**Q13.** A child presented with non-ketotic hypoglycemia. This may be
due to:

A) Hereditary fructose intolerance
B) Hyperinsulinism
C) Von Gierke disease (GSD type I)
D) Galactosemia

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** Non-ketotic hypoglycemia (hypoglycemia WITHOUT elevated ketones) is characteristic of hyperinsulinism (excess insulin suppresses fatty acid oxidation → no ketone production) and fatty acid oxidation disorders (FAO defects). Ketotic hypoglycemia (with ketones) is seen in: Von Gierke disease, galactosemia, hereditary fructose intolerance, and ketotic hypoglycemia of childhood. The absence of ketones is the key clue — it means fatty acid oxidation is impaired (hyperinsulinism) rather than simply inadequate glucose supply.

**Q14.** A newborn infant presents with hypotonia, prominent forehead, large
anterior fontanel, enlarged liver, renal cysts, and markedly increased
plasma very long chain fatty acids (VLCFA). The pathogenesis of this
disorder is due to:

A) Severely reduced numbers of peroxisomes in all cells
B) Abnormal accumulation of one amino acid proximal to a metabolic block
C) Deficiency of a lysosomal enzyme causing accumulation of macromolecules
D) Inborn errors of glycogen synthesis and degradation enzymes

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** The presentation is Zellweger syndrome (Cerebrohepatorenal syndrome) — a severe peroxisomal biogenesis disorder. Zellweger is caused by mutations in PEX genes, leading to severely reduced/absent peroxisomes. Without functional peroxisomes, VLCFA cannot be oxidized (beta-oxidation of VLCFA occurs in peroxisomes) → accumulate in blood and tissues. Features: neonatal hypotonia, craniofacial dysmorphism, hepatomegaly, renal cysts, and absent reflexes. Invariably fatal within the first year of life.

**Q15.** A 6-year-old girl presents with intellectual disability, seizures,
fair hair and skin, and a musty odor of sweat and urine. Regarding this
disorder, which statement is correct?

A) Treatment involves a low-methionine diet with cystine supplementation
B) It is caused by deficiency of the enzyme required for conversion of phenylalanine to tyrosine
C) Dietary fructose restriction gives a good prognosis
D) Diagnosis is confirmed by elevated branched-chain amino acids in blood

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** The presentation describes PKU (phenylketonuria) — caused by phenylalanine hydroxylase (PAH) deficiency, which converts phenylalanine → tyrosine. Without PAH, phenylalanine accumulates → musty/mousy odor, fair skin/hair (tyrosine deficiency → reduced melanin), intellectual disability (untreated). Treatment: low-phenylalanine diet (NOT low-methionine). Low-methionine diet is for homocystinuria. Fructose restriction is for hereditary fructose intolerance. BCAA elevation is MSUD.

**Q16.** A neonate is admitted to the NICU with ammonia level above
200 μmol/L. What disorder is most likely suspected?

A) Glycogen storage disease
B) Urea cycle disorder
C) Mitochondrial disorder
D) Mucopolysaccharidosis

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** Hyperammonemia (elevated ammonia) is the hallmark of urea cycle disorders (UCDs). The urea cycle converts toxic ammonia (NH3) to urea for excretion. Any enzyme defect in the cycle (CPS1, OTC, ASS, ASL, ARG1) causes ammonia accumulation → neonatal encephalopathy, lethargy, vomiting. Severe neonatal hyperammonemia (>200 μmol/L) requires immediate treatment (protein restriction, arginine supplementation, ammonia scavenger drugs, dialysis). Glycogen storage diseases cause hypoglycemia, not hyperammonemia.

**Q17.** A 4-month-old male presents with jaundice since birth, cataract,
and hepatomegaly. Investigations reveal reducing substances in urine.
What is the appropriate management?

A) Prescribing lactose-free (and galactose-free) milk formula
B) Giving tyrosine supplements
C) Restricting leucine and valine
D) Restricting fructose

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** The presentation is classic galactosemia (GALT deficiency): jaundice, cataracts, hepatomegaly, and reducing substances in urine (Clinitest positive = galactose detected but Glucose Oxidase/Clinistix negative). Immediate treatment: eliminate all sources of galactose and lactose from diet → lactose-free galactose-free formula (e.g., soy formula). Delay leads to liver failure, E. coli sepsis, cataracts, and intellectual disability. Tyrosine supplements = alkaptonuria/tyrosinemia treatment. Leucine/valine restriction = MSUD. Fructose restriction = hereditary fructose intolerance.

**Q18.** The laboratory reports significant elevation of plasma arginine.
What additional biochemical abnormality is most expected?

A) Hyperammonemia
B) Lactic acidosis
C) Hypoglycemia
D) Elevated muscle enzymes (CK)

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** Elevated plasma arginine indicates arginase deficiency (ARG1 deficiency) — the last enzyme of the urea cycle, converting arginine → ornithine + urea. When arginase is absent, arginine accumulates AND urea cannot be formed → ammonia accumulates → hyperammonemia. Clinical features of arginase deficiency (UCD): spastic diplegia, intellectual disability, and hyperammonemia (typically milder than other UCDs). Treatment: low-protein diet, arginine-restricted diet, ammonia scavengers.

**Q19.** A 1-year-old boy presents with intellectual disability, fair
skin, and an unusual urine odor. What is the fundamental disease
mechanism in this condition?

A) Energy failure (ATP depletion)
B) Accumulation of large complex molecules (storage)
C) Excess of essential metabolites (substrate deficiency downstream)
D) Accumulation of toxic metabolites (substrate toxicity upstream)

**Answer:** D
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** The presentation is PKU — the disease mechanism is substrate toxicity: phenylalanine (and its abnormal metabolites: phenylpyruvate, phenylacetate, phenyllactate) accumulate to toxic levels → inhibit brain development, neurotransmitter synthesis, and myelin formation. IEM mechanisms include: (1) Substrate toxicity: PKU, MSUD, OA, (2) Product deficiency: albinism (no melanin), (3) Energy failure: mitochondrial disorders, (4) Storage: LSDs (lysosomal storage). PKU specifically = toxic metabolite accumulation.

**Q20.** Which of the following represents the correct disease-diagnostic
biochemical test matching?

A) Mucopolysaccharidosis — Plasma ammonia
B) Homocystinuria — Plasma methionine (and homocysteine)
C) Zellweger syndrome — Plasma glucose
D) Galactosemia — Plasma lactic acid

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** Homocystinuria (CBS deficiency) → elevated plasma methionine and homocysteine (both accumulate due to blocked CBS enzyme). This is the correct match. MPS → elevated urine glycosaminoglycans (not ammonia; ammonia = urea cycle disorder). Zellweger → elevated plasma VLCFA (not glucose). Galactosemia → reducing substances in urine + elevated plasma galactose (not lactic acid; lactic acidosis = mitochondrial disorders or severe hypoxia).

**Q21.** Plasma amino acid analysis is a useful diagnostic tool for
which of the following disorders?

A) Fatty acid oxidation defect
B) Galactosemia
C) Maple syrup urine disease (MSUD)
D) Organic acidemia

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 19
**Explanation:** MSUD (branched-chain alpha-keto acid dehydrogenase deficiency) causes accumulation of branched-chain amino acids (valine, leucine, isoleucine) in plasma → elevated on plasma amino acid analysis. For other conditions: FAO defects → diagnosed by acylcarnitine profile (plasma) and urine organic acids. Galactosemia → reducing substances in urine + RBC enzyme assay. Organic acidemias → urine organic acid analysis (NOT plasma amino acids, though some amino acids may be secondarily affected).

**Q22.** A neonate presents with encephalopathy, vomiting, and plasma
arginine is found to be markedly elevated in addition to elevated ammonia
(400 μmol/L). Which urea cycle disorder is most likely?

A) OTC (ornithine transcarbamylase) deficiency
B) CPS1 deficiency
C) Arginase deficiency (hyperargininemia)
D) Citrullinemia type I

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Arginase deficiency (ARG1 deficiency/hyperargininemia) is the last step of the urea cycle — arginase converts arginine → ornithine + urea. Deficiency → accumulation of ARGININE (markedly elevated plasma arginine) AND failure to complete the urea cycle → hyperammonemia. Unlike other UCDs that typically present as neonatal encephalopathy, arginase deficiency more commonly presents with progressive spastic diplegia and intellectual disability. However, severe forms can present with hyperammonemia in neonates. The combination of elevated arginine + hyperammonemia is the diagnostic clue.

**Q23.** Deficiency of branched-chain alpha-ketoacid dehydrogenase (BCKD)
complex leads to which metabolic disorder?

A) Maple syrup urine disease (MSUD)
B) Phenylketonuria
C) Propionic acidemia
D) Homocystinuria

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Maple Syrup Urine Disease (MSUD) is caused by deficiency of the branched-chain alpha-ketoacid dehydrogenase (BCKD) complex — a mitochondrial multienzyme complex that catabolizes the keto-acid derivatives of all three branched-chain amino acids (BCAAs): leucine, isoleucine, and valine. Deficiency → accumulation of BCAAs and their corresponding alpha-keto acids → maple syrup/sweet odor of urine → neonatal encephalopathy, cerebral edema, seizures, and death if untreated. Treatment: BCAA-restricted diet + thiamine supplementation.

**Q24.** A patient with homocystinuria is started on the appropriate
dietary management. Which diet is correct for treating this condition?

A) Low-phenylalanine diet with tyrosine supplementation
B) Low-galactose and lactose-free diet
C) Low-methionine diet with cysteine supplementation
D) Low-branched-chain amino acid diet with thiamine

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Homocystinuria (CBS — cystathionine beta-synthase deficiency) treatment: (1) Low-methionine diet (methionine is the dietary precursor to homocysteine — reducing intake lowers homocysteine accumulation), (2) Cysteine supplementation (cysteine becomes an essential amino acid because CBS is needed for its synthesis), (3) Pyridoxine (B6) supplementation for pyridoxine-responsive forms (reduces homocysteine by enhancing residual CBS activity), and (4) Betaine (methyl donor for homocysteine remethylation). Low-phenylalanine = PKU; low-galactose = galactosemia; low-BCAA = MSUD.
