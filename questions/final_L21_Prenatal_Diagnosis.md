**Q1.** An increased maternal serum beta-hCG (B-hCG) combined with
a reduced maternal serum AFP (MSAFP) suggests a possibility of which
condition?

A) Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
B) Hydatidiform mole
C) Neural tube defect
D) Edwards syndrome (Trisomy 18)

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** The triple/quadruple screening test pattern for Down syndrome (Trisomy 21): elevated B-hCG (↑), reduced MSAFP (↓), low uE3 (↓), elevated inhibin A (↑). Neural tube defects show markedly ELEVATED MSAFP (not reduced). Edwards syndrome (Trisomy 18) shows low hCG + low MSAFP + low uE3. Hydatidiform mole causes extremely high hCG but is not a fetal chromosomal abnormality.

**Q2.** Increased maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein (MSAFP) suggests a
possibility of which condition?

A) Neural tube defect (open NTD)
B) Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
C) Edwards syndrome (Trisomy 18)
D) Turner syndrome

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Elevated MSAFP indicates leakage of fetal AFP into maternal blood, most commonly from an open neural tube defect (NTD) such as anencephaly or meningomyelocele (spina bifida aperta). Other causes of elevated MSAFP: gastroschisis, abdominal wall defects, fetal death, multiple pregnancy. Down syndrome and other trisomies typically show REDUCED MSAFP. Elevated MSAFP should be followed by detailed fetal ultrasound and possibly amniocentesis for amniotic AFP.

**Q3.** Which of the following is an indication for prenatal diagnosis?

A) Very young maternal age (teenage pregnancy)
B) Advanced maternal age (≥35 years)
C) Family history of obesity
D) Smoking during pregnancy

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Indications for prenatal diagnosis include: advanced maternal age (≥35 years) due to increased risk of chromosomal aneuploidy (especially trisomy 21), previous child with chromosomal abnormality, carrier status for chromosomal rearrangement or single-gene disorder, abnormal maternal serum screening, family history of genetic condition diagnosable prenatally, and abnormal fetal ultrasound findings. Obesity, smoking, and young maternal age are NOT standard indications for invasive prenatal testing.

**Q4.** A 13-week pregnant woman has low PAPP-A and low beta-hCG
on first-trimester screening. What is the best confirmatory diagnostic
test?

A) Triple test (second trimester)
B) Quadruple test (second trimester)
C) Chorionic villus sampling (CVS)
D) No further testing needed

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 24
**Explanation:** At 13 weeks, chorionic villus sampling (CVS) is the appropriate invasive diagnostic test for suspected chromosomal abnormality. CVS is performed between 10-14 weeks of gestation, providing a definitive karyotype/chromosomal diagnosis. Low PAPP-A + low beta-hCG in first trimester is a pattern seen in chromosomal aneuploidy (particularly trisomies). The triple and quadruple tests are second-trimester screening tests — not indicated when the question asks for confirmatory diagnosis at 13 weeks.

**Q5.** A 13-week pregnant woman has low PAPP-A and low beta-hCG.
What is the most likely underlying fetal condition?

A) Aneuploidy (chromosomal abnormality)
B) Single-gene disorder
C) Multifactorial condition
D) Normal variant

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 24
**Explanation:** Low PAPP-A (pregnancy-associated plasma protein-A) combined with low beta-hCG at 11-13 weeks is characteristic of fetal aneuploidy — particularly trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome) and trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome). Trisomy 21 typically shows elevated hCG + reduced PAPP-A. Monosomy X (Turner) shows low PAPP-A + low hCG. Single-gene disorders and multifactorial conditions do not typically affect these serum markers.

**Q6.** The image shows a procedure performed on a pregnant woman
for fetal genetic testing. What is the best description of this
procedure?

A) Chorionic villus sampling (invasive)
B) Invasive prenatal test
C) Non-invasive prenatal test (NIPT/cfDNA)
D) Amniocentesis
E) Cordocentesis

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 24
**Explanation:** Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT) uses cell-free fetal DNA (cfDNA) circulating in maternal blood, obtained by a simple maternal blood draw. No risk to the fetus. NIPT screens for trisomy 21, 18, 13, and sex chromosome aneuploidies with high sensitivity/specificity. It is a SCREENING test (not diagnostic) — positive results require confirmation by CVS or amniocentesis. The image shows a blood draw from the mother, distinguishing it from invasive procedures.

**Q7.** A 13-week pregnant woman has low PAPP-A and low beta-hCG.
Suspected trisomy. What is the best confirmatory diagnostic test?

A) Quadruple test
B) Triple test
C) Chorionic villus sampling (CVS)
D) Screening alone is sufficient

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 24
**Explanation:** When first-trimester screening (PAPP-A + beta-hCG + nuchal translucency) is abnormal and trisomy is suspected at 13 weeks, the best CONFIRMATORY diagnostic test is CVS (10-14 weeks) — it provides a definitive fetal karyotype. Amniocentesis is performed at 15-20 weeks. Triple and quadruple tests are second-trimester screening tools, not diagnostic. Screening alone is never sufficient when confirmation is required for clinical decision-making.

**Q8.** What is the recurrence risk for siblings when a child is born
with bilateral cleft lip and palate?

A) 4.2%
B) 2.6%
C) 5.6%
D) 0.5%

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** Cleft lip and palate (CL/P) is a multifactorial condition. Recurrence risk increases with: severity (bilateral > unilateral), number of affected family members, and severity of the proband's defect. For bilateral cleft lip and palate (most severe form): recurrence risk for siblings is approximately 5–6%. For unilateral CL/P: sibling risk ~3-4%. For cleft palate alone: sibling risk ~2-3%. The answer of 5.6% reflects the higher recurrence risk associated with bilateral involvement.

**Q9.** Which medication is recommended during the first trimester of
pregnancy to reduce the risk of neural tube defects?

A) Streptomycin
B) Folic acid
C) Isoniazid
D) Valproic acid

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** Periconceptional folic acid supplementation (ideally starting 1 month before conception through the first trimester) reduces neural tube defect (NTD) risk by 50–70%. The neural tube closes during weeks 3–4 of gestation (before many women know they are pregnant), so supplementation must begin BEFORE conception. Streptomycin is ototoxic (teratogen). Isoniazid carries risks. Valproic acid is a known NTD-inducing teratogen (it inhibits folate metabolism) — actually increases NTD risk.

**Q10.** Two parents have two affected children with Hemophilia A. They
want a healthy child. What is the best recommendation?

A) Preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD)
B) Newborn screening after birth
C) Early diagnosis and treatment only
D) Chorionic villus sampling in each pregnancy

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** Preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) combined with in-vitro fertilization (IVF) allows genetic testing of embryos BEFORE implantation. For Hemophilia A (X-linked recessive), PGD can identify unaffected embryos (carrier females or unaffected males) for implantation, ensuring the couple has a healthy child while avoiding the ethical issues of prenatal diagnosis followed by possible pregnancy termination. Newborn screening detects disease after birth (too late for prevention). CVS is prenatal diagnosis, not preimplantation.

**Q11.** An increase in inhibin-A in maternal serum is associated with:

A) Cystic fibrosis
B) Down syndrome (Trisomy 21)
C) Turner syndrome
D) Duchenne muscular dystrophy

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** The quadruple test (second trimester, 15-20 weeks) for Down syndrome includes: AFP (low ↓), hCG (high ↑), unconjugated estriol/uE3 (low ↓), and inhibin-A (HIGH ↑). Elevated inhibin-A is one of the four markers in the quad screen. The elevated inhibin-A in trisomy 21 pregnancies is thought to be due to abnormal placental function associated with the extra chromosome 21. Inhibin-A is NOT elevated in cystic fibrosis, Turner syndrome, or DMD.

**Q12.** A mother has one child with galactosemia and is now pregnant
with her second child in the second trimester. What genetic test is
most appropriate to advise?

A) Newborn screening after birth
B) Parental carrier testing
C) Ultrasonography only
D) Maternal serum AFP

**Answer:** B
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** When a couple has already had a child with an autosomal recessive condition (galactosemia), both parents are confirmed obligate carriers. Parental carrier testing confirms carrier status and allows calculation of recurrence risk (25% for each subsequent pregnancy). The appropriate prenatal diagnostic test for the CURRENT pregnancy would be CVS or amniocentesis for enzyme assay or molecular testing. Newborn screening identifies affected babies after birth (doesn't help plan the current pregnancy).

**Q13.** Which prenatal test does NOT carry a risk of fetal loss?

A) Maternal serum AFP (MSAFP)
B) Chorionic villus sampling (CVS)
C) Fetal tissue sampling
D) Amniocentesis

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** Maternal serum AFP is a maternal blood test — it poses no risk whatsoever to the fetus (only a blood draw from the mother). Invasive prenatal tests that carry fetal loss risk: CVS (~0.5-1% procedure-related fetal loss), amniocentesis (~0.1-0.3% fetal loss), and fetal tissue sampling (higher risk). The distinction between invasive (CVS, amniocentesis, cordocentesis) and non-invasive (maternal blood tests, ultrasound, NIPT) prenatal tests is clinically important.

**Q14.** The triple screen test includes all of the following EXCEPT:

A) Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP)
B) Human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG)
C) Unconjugated estriol (uE3)
D) Indirect bilirubin

**Answer:** D
**Source:** Med 18
**Explanation:** The triple screen (second trimester, 15-20 weeks) consists of three markers: AFP (from fetal liver/yolk sac), hCG (from placenta), and unconjugated estriol/uE3 (from fetal adrenals → placenta → maternal blood). The quadruple screen adds a 4th marker: inhibin-A. Indirect bilirubin is NOT part of prenatal screening tests. It is measured in newborns for jaundice assessment or in adults for hemolytic anemia evaluation.

**Q15.** Which of the following is classified as a non-invasive prenatal
diagnostic/screening method?

A) Amniocentesis (amniotic fluid sampling)
B) Chorionic villus sampling (CVS)
C) Three-dimensional (3D) ultrasound
D) Cordocentesis (fetal blood sampling)

**Answer:** C
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** 3D ultrasound (and standard 2D ultrasound) is a non-invasive prenatal method — it uses sound waves with no penetration of the uterine or fetal membranes, posing no risk of fetal loss. Non-invasive methods include: ultrasound (2D, 3D, 4D), maternal serum screening, and NIPT (cfDNA blood test). Invasive methods requiring needle insertion: amniocentesis (~0.1–0.3% fetal loss risk), CVS (~0.5–1% fetal loss risk), and cordocentesis (higher risk). Non-invasive tests are screening tools; invasive tests provide definitive diagnosis.

**Q16.** In prenatal diagnosis, which approach involves testing of
embryos before implantation to avoid transmission of a genetic disorder?

A) Preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) via IVF
B) First-trimester chorionic villus sampling
C) Second-trimester amniocentesis
D) Non-invasive prenatal testing (NIPT)

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** Preimplantation genetic diagnosis (PGD) — now termed preimplantation genetic testing (PGT) — involves testing embryos created via IVF BEFORE uterine implantation. Only genetically unaffected embryos are transferred, preventing disease transmission entirely. This is the earliest possible form of prenatal genetic testing and avoids the ethical dilemma of terminating an affected pregnancy. It is applicable to single-gene disorders, chromosomal abnormalities, and HLA matching. First-line choice for couples who want a healthy child without pregnancy termination.

**Q17.** A DNA fingerprinting (STR profiling) study of a family shows
four children. The DNA profile of Child 4 does not share any alleles
with the alleged father's profile. What is the most likely interpretation?

A) Child 4 is adopted (not biologically related to the alleged father)
B) Child 4 has a new de novo mutation altering all STR markers
C) The DNA sample was contaminated
D) Child 4 has uniparental disomy from the mother only

**Answer:** A
**Source:** Med 25
**Explanation:** DNA fingerprinting using short tandem repeat (STR) markers establishes biological relationships. Each person inherits one STR allele from each parent. If a child shares NO alleles with the alleged father across multiple STR loci, this definitively excludes biological paternity. The most likely explanation is that Child 4 is not biologically related to the alleged father (e.g., adopted or has a different biological father). De novo mutations affecting multiple STR loci simultaneously is extremely unlikely. Contamination would typically show extra/inconsistent bands, not a completely different profile.
